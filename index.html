Introduction

Civ 5 is considered to be one of the best selling grand strategy games ever made. Not just due to it's IP recognition, but it's detailed grand strategy mechanics. This game alone has inspired a majority of the grand strategy games the past decade. This is a quick guice for beginners on how the game works.

Quick Facts

Some quick facts about the game:

  1. One of the first strategy games to use the hex grid pattern, instead of the traditional square grid.
  2. Still more played and beloved than Civilization 6.
  3. Introduced many new concepts that are used by other strategy games.
  4. One of the most profitable IPs.
Civilizations and Leaders

You first pick a country that comes with it's own leader. The leaders each have their own abilities. Some abilites can be overpowered, while some are incredibly situational. An example of this is Japan's Oda Nobunaga's abilty. It allows all Japanese units to fight at full power. Even if the HP is 1, it would still fight as if it were still 100.

Unique Units and Buildings

Each civilization has a unique building and or unit. They provide bonuses that other civilizations' buildings and units can't. As a result, one has to strategize what Civilization to use for what victory. Even then, you have to become a tactitian during gameplay; you need to actively think ahead of your opponents and even during situations that are occuring.

Basics

The object of the game is to achieve one of five victories:

  1. Science: Be the first to build and unite all spaceship parts to fly to Alpha Centari.
  2. Domination: Capture all the capitals of all other civilizations.
  3. Cultural: Achieve 100% cultural influence in all civilizations.
  4. Diplomacy: Become World Leader in the World Congress
  5. Score: If none of the above victories are achieved by the year 2050 or time limit, then the player with the highest score wins.
Ancient Ruins

Ancient ruins are special tiles that are critical in early game. They give a random minor boost that makes a huge difference as you're starting off. However, if an enemy unit lands on them, they get the bonus, so be quick.

Barbarians

During Early gameply, they are as much of a danger to you than enemy civilizations, because they immediately attack you and destroy your resource tiles.

Pantheon and Religion

Although pantheons and religions aren't important for most civilizations, it can be crucial that some are able to get them. There can only be 7 unique religions during a game and each country is racing to get a Great Prophet before others. If they don't they'll lose their chance at a religion. However, if they do get a religion, all the good bonuses may already be taken.

Resource Tiles

Resource tiles are literally what they are, resource tiles. Whether the tile is a food tile or a luxary tile, they each are added towards their respective calculations. Each tile carries a number of resources that add to the calculations. For example, let's say you have multiple food tiles. This would be the calculations:

  • n: The number of citizens in the city
  • f: Amount of food to grow to size n+1
  • f(n)=15+6(n−1)+(n−1)1.8 rounded down to the next integer.
Technology

Technology is how you advance through the eras and get improved buildings, units, and improvements. You technology should never be ignored, because it won't be good if your enemies are advancing their cannons to you when you're still using catapults. There are also bonuses for some Civilizations when they reach the next era or if they're the first ones to reach the next era.

Culture

Culture is needed for a Culture Victory, but in the short run, you need it for purchasing Social Policies. These policies are what gives your Civilization bonuses and can open up the oppertunity to build a World Wonder.

  • n: The number of cities in your empire
  • pbase(k): The base cost of policy k. Depends only on the number of policies
  • pcities(n): The cost factor depending on the number of cities in your empire
  • p(n,k): The total cost of policy k. Depends on both the number of cities and policies already unlocked

    • The policy cost has to be modified depending on your difficulty setting, game speed and map size. I took the numbers from a post by Yamian. Define these modifiers as:

  • mt: The game speed modifier (3 for marathon, 1.5 for epic, 1 for normal and 0.67 for quick)
  • md: The difficulty coefficient (0.5 for settler, 0.67 for chieftain, 0.85 for warlord, 1 otherwise)
  • mz: The map size modifier (0.3 for normal size and below, 0.2 for large, 0.15 for huge)
  • pbase(k)=mtmd(25+(6k)1.7)=mtmd(25+21.03k1.7)
  • cities(n)=1+mz(n−1)

  • All in all, this will lead to the final calculation:

  • p(n,k)=pbase(k)∗pcities(n) rounded to the next multiple of 5
Military

Each unit, regardless of military, worker, or settler; are added into the unit calculations when a new one is added or killed.

  • b = Base unit cost (INITIAL_GOLD_PER_UNIT_TIMES_100)
  • f = Free units from handicap (GoldFreeUnits), specific unit types, or policies
  • u = Total paid units
  • n = max(0, u-f) = Number of actual paid units (if u-f is less than 0 then n = 0)
  • m = Multiplier (UNIT_MAINTENANCE_GAME_MULTIPLIER)
  • d = Divisor (UNIT_MAINTENANCE_GAME_EXPONENT_DIVISOR)
  • t = Current turn
  • e = Estimated end turn (based on all entries for the current GameSpeed in GameSpeed_Turns)
  • g = t/e = Game progress factor
  • final cost = (n*b(1+g*m)/100)(1+(g/d))
GDP

This is your countries money. It helps calculate how much money you are making each turn, but also shows how much each unit, tile, and luxary are either helping or hurting you. It is up to you to keep the GDP high or else your civilization's happiness will begin to drop.

Great Person

A Greate Person is an histroical figure whom can help you in gameplay. They can either provide a tile improvement that improves your civilization over time, or they can provide an immediate huge improvement if you are desperate for it. However, they are still considered units, so they will die in battle if an enemy unit attacks them enough times.

Eras

The game has 8 eras. Each era opens new technologies to reseatch, buildings to build, military units to train, and wonders to create. These era are:

  1. Ancient
  2. Classical
  3. Medival
  4. Rennasiance
  5. Industrial
  6. Modern
  7. Atomic
  8. Information
